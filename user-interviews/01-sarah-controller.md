# Interview: Sarah Chen, Corporate Controller

**Role**: Corporate Controller
**Tenure**: 8 years at company, 15 years in accounting
**Date**: January 15, 2026

---

## Opening

**Interviewer**: Walk me through what month-end close looks like for your team.

**Sarah**: We have 8 days to close, but the heavy lifting is days 1-5. My team of 6 accountants each owns different areas. The big bottleneck is reconciling 180 balance sheet accounts—45 of them are "critical" requiring detailed analysis, documentation, and my personal sign-off.

The process is controlled chaos. On Day 1, we're pulling data from multiple systems. Days 2-4, the team is reconciling and investigating variances. Days 4-5, I'm reviewing and sending back 30-40% for rework. Days 6-8, we're doing final approvals and fire drills.

---

## Core Pain Points

**Interviewer**: What takes the most time?

**Sarah**: Three things consume us:

**First: Variance investigation**. When an account moves unexpectedly, someone needs to figure out why. That's detective work—pulling transaction details, talking to business partners, reviewing contracts, checking prior periods. My senior accountant Jake is brilliant at this, but he spends 60% of his time just hunting down explanations.

**Second: Documentation completeness and quality**. For SOX compliance, every critical reconciliation needs supporting docs. But what constitutes "support" varies by account. Chasing down the right documentation, ensuring it's current, ensuring it ties to numbers—incredibly manual.

I reject 30-40% of reconciliations on first submission. Not because the accounting is wrong—usually the numbers are fine—but because the **story isn't clear** or **backup is incomplete**. "Why did this go up $400K?" isn't answered, or the supporting invoice is missing.

**Third: Coordination overhead**. Month-end touches everyone—Sales Ops, Treasury, IT, Facilities, HR. We're constantly chasing confirmations: "Do you have any unpaid invoices?" "Can you explain this allocation?" The back-and-forth eats days.

---

## What Good Looks Like

**Interviewer**: When you see a really good reconciliation, what stands out?

**Sarah**: It tells a coherent story proactively. The numbers tie, obviously. But beyond that:

- **Explains unusual items upfront**: Don't make me ask "Why did this go up $400K?"—tell me: "Professional services accrual increased $400K due to Accenture engagement on ERP project (PO #45782, see attached SOW). This was anticipated per project plan."

- **References prior periods and trends**: "This is consistent with Q4 pattern from last year when we also had elevated consulting spend." Shows the preparer understands the account's behavior.

- **Purposeful documentation**: Not data dumps, but the right artifacts supporting key judgments.

- **Completeness assertion**: Evidence that we asked "Did we miss anything?" and have documented answers.

---

## Wish List

**Interviewer**: If you had a magic wand...

**Sarah**: A few things:

**One**: Automatic variance investigation. Not deciding the accounting treatment—I need human judgment there—but the detective work. If something is unusual, automatically surface related transactions, relevant contracts, prior period comparisons. Give the team a head start.

**Two**: Institutional memory. We've reconciled these accounts dozens of times. There are patterns. Prepaid expenses behaves a certain way. Intercompany has recurring issues. But each month we start from scratch. The system should learn.

**Three**: Proactive documentation. The system knows we'll need the amortization schedule for prepaid expenses. Why not suggest it or auto-attach the latest version?

**Four**: Draft explanations. Based on transaction detail and supporting docs, could AI draft the narrative? Even if it's 70% right and we edit, that would save hours.

---

## Audit & Compliance Requirements

**Interviewer**: What are your non-negotiables from a compliance perspective?

**Sarah**: We're SOX 404(b) compliant, audited by Big 4. That means:

**Audit trail and accountability**: I need to show "a qualified human reviewed this and signed off." No black boxes.

**Evidence contemporaneously prepared**: Documentation created at the time of close, not after the fact when auditors ask for it.

**Segregation of duties**: Preparer ≠ Reviewer. Reviewer has appropriate authority level.

**Completeness procedures**: For liability accounts especially, we must show we asked business partners to confirm no missing obligations.

**Support for judgments and estimates**: If we're estimating an accrual, we need documented methodology, assumptions, comparison to actuals when available.

I've seen vendors promise "AI-powered close" that's just keyword search. We need something that maintains audit quality while reducing work.

---

## Concerns About Automation

**Interviewer**: What worries you about AI in this process?

**Sarah**: **Edge cases**. Accounting has so many nuances. A $5M transaction might be routine, or it might be a related party transaction needing special disclosure. Context matters. I don't want confident wrong answers.

**Change management**. My team is already drowning during close. Introducing tools that break workflows could make things worse before better.

**Control deficiencies**. If automation means inconsistent documentation or missing evidence, we'll have audit findings. That's career-ending for finance leadership.

But I'm not anti-technology. I'm pro-technology that genuinely helps without creating new risks.

---

## Success Metrics

**Interviewer**: How would you measure success?

**Sarah**:
- **Days to close**: 8 → 5 days would be transformational
- **Review cycle time**: If reconciliations come to me complete the first time, I can approve faster
- **First-time approval rate**: Currently 60%, if we get to 85%+ that's huge
- **Audit findings**: Zero control deficiencies related to reconciliations
- **Team satisfaction**: Month-end shouldn't be this painful

---

## Closing

**Interviewer**: Anything else?

**Sarah**: This is a real problem worth solving. Finance teams everywhere deal with this. Tools haven't kept up with complexity.

But please don't over-engineer it. I need something genuinely helpful, not just technically impressive. Make it reduce work while maintaining quality.

And keep humans in control. This is a judgment-heavy domain with regulatory oversight. Assist my team, don't try to replace them.

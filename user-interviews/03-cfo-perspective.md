# Interview: Michael Torres, CFO

**Role**: Chief Financial Officer
**Tenure**: 2 years at company, 20 years finance leadership
**Date**: January 20, 2026

---

## Strategic Context

**Interviewer**: What's your perspective on the close process?

**Michael**: I'll be direct. We're at an inflection point. We went public in 2022, and scrutiny is intense. Every day of delay in close is a day we can't provide guidance to investors or make strategic decisions with confidence.

But here's the tension: I also need **quality over speed**. We had a restatement scare in Q2 2024—nothing material, but it cost us credibility. So when Sarah says she needs more time to "get it right," I support that. But it can't take 8 days.

The bigger issue isn't just close speed. It's **decision-useful information**. By the time we close the books, the information is stale. I need real-time visibility into business drivers, not just historical accounting.

---

## Different Priority: Business Intelligence

**Interviewer**: What would be most valuable to you?

**Michael**: Honestly? I care less about reconciliation automation and more about **predictive analytics and business insights**.

Can we predict month-end results on Day 20 instead of Day 8 of the *next* month? Can we identify revenue at risk before quarter ends? Can we spot expense overruns in real-time so budget owners can course-correct?

The accounting team is drowning in compliance work—SOX controls, audit support, reconciliation documentation. That's table stakes. But it's not value-add to the business.

I'd rather invest in tools that give us **forward-looking insights** than tools that make backward-looking compliance 20% faster.

---

## Forecasting Use Case

**Interviewer**: Can you be more specific about the forecasting need?

**Michael**: Yes. Here's what matters to me:

By **Day 20 of each month**, I need to know: Are we hitting revenue and expense targets? If not, what's driving variance and can we do anything about it?

Right now that forecast comes from FP&A, and it's not reliable. They're guessing based on pipeline data. The accounting team has better data—actual transactions to date—but they don't synthesize it into a forecast.

Imagine an AI that:
- Analyzes transaction trends through Day 20
- Compares to prior month and prior year patterns
- Predicts where we'll land for full month
- Identifies anomalies or risks

That gives me **10 days of runway** to make decisions. Way more valuable than saving 2 days on close.

---

## Risk Tolerance & Disagreement with Sarah

**Interviewer**: Sarah mentioned concerns about automation. Your thoughts?

**Michael**: *pauses* Here's where Sarah and I sometimes disagree.

She's risk-averse—protecting the company from audit findings and restatements. But sometimes I think we're **over-controlling**. Do we really need a human reviewing every single reconciliation? Can't we use risk-based approaches—automate low-risk stuff, focus human review on high-risk areas?

I'm comfortable with more automation if designed properly. Sarah wants humans in the loop on everything. That's expensive and doesn't scale.

We're hiring in EMEA and APAC. Do I want to add more accountants? Or can we use technology to scale without headcount?

---

## Investment Prioritization

**Interviewer**: If you had to choose between close acceleration vs. forecasting vs. making accountants' lives easier, what would you fund?

**Michael**: Tough question. *thinks*

**Tier 1**: Business intelligence and forecasting. This is transformational. Help me run the business better.

**Tier 2**: Close acceleration, but only if it doesn't compromise quality. I need both speed AND accuracy. No shortcuts on SOX or audit readiness.

**Tier 3**: Making accountants' lives easier. I care about team satisfaction, but that's not my primary objective. If automating reconciliations frees up time—great. But I want that time redirected to value-add analysis, not just "less stress."

I know that sounds harsh, but I'm accountable to the board and investors. They don't care if Jake works until midnight. They care if we hit numbers and make smart decisions.

---

## Technology Philosophy

**Interviewer**: What's your philosophy on AI in finance?

**Michael**: Bullish but pragmatic.

I've seen vendors demo "AI-powered close" that's just keyword search and RPA. That's not AI.

Real AI should:
1. **Learn from patterns** - understand context, not just apply rules
2. **Handle ambiguity** - make judgment calls on fuzzy situations
3. **Explain reasoning** - transparent, not black box
4. **Improve over time** - get smarter with more data

I'm willing to invest in that. But I'm not interested in point solutions solving one narrow problem. I want **platforms** that transform how finance operates.

---

## Balancing Sarah's Priorities

**Interviewer**: How do you balance your priorities with Sarah's concerns?

**Michael**: Sarah and I have different lenses:
- **She optimizes for risk mitigation**. I optimize for value creation.
- **She wants more documentation**. I want less manual work and more insight.
- **She's cautious about automation**. I'm aggressive about technology adoption.

This tension is healthy—we balance each other. But any solution needs to satisfy both:
- **Sarah needs**: Audit trail, human oversight, SOX compliance, quality assurance
- **I need**: Speed, scalability, decision-useful insights, ROI

That's a hard problem. Most vendors pick a lane. We need something that does both.

---

## Success Metrics (CFO View)

**Interviewer**: How would you measure success?

**Michael**: Different from Sarah's metrics:

1. **Days to decision-useful information** - Not just close, but when can I act on data?
2. **Forecast accuracy** - Can we predict month-end results by Day 20 within 5%?
3. **Cost to close** - Total labor hours (should go down)
4. **Headcount scalability** - Can we 2x revenue without 2x-ing accounting team?
5. **Business insights generated** - Are we identifying opportunities/risks faster?

Sarah's metrics (days to close, review cycles, team satisfaction) are operational. Mine are strategic.

---

## Budget & Expectations

**Interviewer**: What's your appetite for investment?

**Michael**: If someone can deliver real value—not marginal improvement—I'll fund it.

For a solution that cuts close time 8 → 5 days AND improves forecast accuracy AND scales with growth? I'd invest **$500K-$1M in year 1**, expecting ROI within 18 months.

But I need **proof of concept fast**. Don't spend 6 months building perfection. Show me an MVP in 6-8 weeks solving one high-value problem exceptionally well. Then iterate.

I'm skeptical of big enterprise implementations. We're doing SAP with Accenture now and it's painful. I want agile, iterative development.

---

## Advice for Solution Design

**Interviewer**: Any guidance for someone building a solution?

**Michael**: A few things:

1. **Don't just automate current process**. Reimagine it. Maybe reconciliations don't need to be this manual.

2. **Think about the system, not just the task**. How does it connect to forecasting, variance analysis, audit, management reporting?

3. **Design for scale**. We're $295M today, targeting $500M in 3 years. Will it work at 2x volume?

4. **Measure outcomes, not outputs**. I don't care how many reconciliations get automated. I care if close time decreases and quality improves.

5. **Get CFO buy-in, not just controller buy-in**. Sarah controls the process, but I control the budget. You need both.

---

## Closing

**Interviewer**: Anything else?

**Michael**: The opportunity in finance is huge. We're using 1990s processes with 2010s tools. AI can be transformational.

But don't build another feature creating more work under the guise of "helping." Sarah's team is maxed out. Anything new must **genuinely reduce workload** while improving outputs.

And don't forget business context. We're not just trying to close faster. We're running a growing, complex business in a competitive market. Help us do that.
